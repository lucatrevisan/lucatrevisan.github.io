Projects

Each student taking the course for credit must select a final project. The requirements for a project are to read papers
related to a topic not covered (or not covered deeply) in class, and to give a 25 minutes presentation. A written report is not required, but may be submitted as part of the project. Presentations will be scheduled in the week of May 15-18 (I will
probably be out of town monday May 14), possibly concentrated between May 16 and 17.

Below is a list of possible topics for projects. If you have been particularly interested in a topic that was only briefly developed in class, you are encouraged to propose it as your project topic.
 

  • Relations between interactive proof models (Alison, Mark):
    • Transformation of general protocols into public-coin protocols (Goldwasser-Sipser)
    • Complexity of black-box transformation of general protocols into public-coin protocols (Vadhan)
    • "Speed-up theorem" for public-coin protocols (Babai-Moran)
  • Pseudorandom generators and one-way functions
    • Transformation of "regular" one-way functions into pseudorandom generators (Goldreich-Krawczyk-Luby). In this case, it would be particularly good to have a written report with explicit calculations of the relation between the security of the construction and the security of the one-way function.
  • Pseudorandom permutations (John)
    • Construction of pseudorandom functions from pseudorandom generators (Goldreich-Goldwasser-Micali)
    • Construction of pseudorandom permutations from pseudorandom functions (Luby-Rackoff)
    • (Optional) Improved construction of pseudorandom permutations from pseudorandom functions (Naor-Reingold)
  • Low-degree test
    • "Simple" analysis in Rubinfeld-Sudan
    • Analysis for the bivariate case in Arora-Safra (or Polischuck-Spielman)
    • (Optional) Reduction of the general case to the bivariate case (Arora-Safra and ALMSS)
  • Hardness of approximation (Kunal)
    • Reductions from Hastad's verifier to Max CUT, Max 2SAT, etc. (Trevisan-Sorkin-Sudan-Williamson)
    • Hardness of Set Cover (Feige)
  • One-sided error vs. two-sided error in randomized computations and combinatorial constructions (Jordan)
    • Simulation of randomized algorithms using a weak random source and a disperser (Andreev-Clementi-Rolim-Trevisan)
    • Deterministic simulation of BPP using deterministic simulation of promise-RP (Burhman-Fortnow, and Goldreich-Vadhan-Wigderson)
  • More constructions of extractors using the NW generator
    • Use of weak designs (Raz-Reingold-Vadhan)
    • "Win-win" recursive analysis (Impagliazzo-Shaltiel-Wigderson)
    • (Optional) Construction of "condensers" (TaShma-Umans-Zuckerman)
  • Average-case hardness of the permanent
    • Hardness for an exponentially small subset of the domain (Feige-Lund)
  • Error-correcting codes (Lawrence, Ranjit)
    • Expander codes (Sipser-Spielman) and super-concentrator codes (Spielman)
  • Time-space trade-offs for SAT
    • SAT cannot be solved in time n^{1+o(1)} and space n^{1-epsilon} (Fortnow)
    • SAT cannot be solved in time n^{1.618} and space n^{o(1)} (Fortnow-Melkebeek)
  • Average-case analysis of the simplex algorithm (Chris)
  • Existence of random-self-reductions for NP-complete problems (Dror)
  • Complexity of the circuit minimization problem (Scott)
